Rubric

Points Phrase Definition

1

Poor

These sites lack responsiveness, offer poor accessibility, lack in any redeeming artistic effects or general usability. Even plain HTML sites usually rank above this, reserving this score for spaghetti pages made in an attempt to imitate flashier pages with no substance to back it.

2

Mediocre

Mediocre sites have few glaring issues, but may lack in a meaningful DOM, have distracting CSS formatting in-line styled, have scattered designs, and may lack a sense of purpose. Long personal blog sites made from scratch without direction may tend towards this.

3

Average

Average sites are those that fulfill the basic needs of any decent site: their HTML is mostly semantic, leaving aesthetics to CSS, and said aesthetics are not distracting or wrongly done, though perhaps plain and ascetic. Pages have a structure and flow, and things are where they logically should be: navigation is likely at the side or top, pictures perhaps take up limited space, etc. A plain but well-designed scratch site may fall into this category.

4

Great

Great sites are those designed with purpose and research. UX is studied and implemented throughout the site, with obvious attention to where the user is directed and how they flow through the site. Attention is made to being mindful of space and resources, such that pages do not take too long to load and the user is not overwhelmed (and, coincidentally, the SEO ranking may go up). There may be some visual flourishes, but only in aid of the user's and/or site's goal (for instance, a submit button may be highlighted or stand-out from the clear to avoid issues). Alternatively, great sites can be classified as those who fulfill their purpose in another manner. For example, a portfolio or artistic site that maximizes on visual effects of awesome, even at the expense of speed and loading.

5

Purposeful

The highest-rated sites are reserved for those that either break new ground in what's possible for a website or have extremely fine-tuned and excellent design. Such sites must be near flawless or have extraordinary capability in fulfilling their purpose: a portfolio that masterfully shows off the designers potential or a simple but effective and streamlined shopping site.

Reflection

First, I learned that HTML is somewhat dumb and inconsistent. Some form elements are handled by input type="", whereas others are handled with their own elements (e.g. select), which seems counter-intuitive to a streamlined standard. Secondly, I learned the differences bewteen GET and POST. Originally each had their own seperate uses which one can probably derive from their names. However, both can be used in relatively similar capacities of passing data from a user now. POST, overall, seems to be the superior method in many cases due to its lack of length limit, ability to be secured and encrypted, etc. GET, however, does serve some use, and notably boasts the ability to have a URL that can display the same results to multiple users (whereas POST does not share its data in links and thus requires more handiwork to replicate results). Regarding styling, I simply linked my stylesheet from previous projects that normalized the styling. I already had a table styling on my "Examples" page, hence not too much extra work was needed. The CSS alternates childs to apply a slightly different opacity/background-color to give each row distinction, as well as a on-hover effect to give some "pop". Ideally, the rubric and examples page will join forces, with the form submitting data to a server and the page being generated by that server, which is completely do-able, except I dont have an active server -- an alternative would thus be Javascript loading a local text file an using it for persistent memory, something worth considering in the future.
